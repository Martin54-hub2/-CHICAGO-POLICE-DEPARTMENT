Community Policing Strategic Plans

Building trust through partnership and collaboration

Our Vision

"The Chicago Police Department envisions a city where police and community members work together as partners to identify problems, develop solutions, and build the trust necessary for safe, thriving neighborhoods."

Community policing is not a program—it's a philosophy that guides everything we do. It requires a fundamental shift from reactive law enforcement to proactive problem-solving, from isolated police work to collaborative partnerships, and from measuring success by arrests to measuring it by community safety and quality of life.

The Four Pillars of Community Policing

🤝 Community Partnerships

Building authentic, ongoing relationships with residents, businesses, schools, faith organizations, and community groups to address shared concerns and co-create solutions.

🔍 Problem-Solving

Using data, community input, and the SARA model (Scanning, Analysis, Response, Assessment) to identify root causes of crime and disorder and develop targeted interventions.

🏘️ Organizational Change

Restructuring policies, training, and accountability systems to support community policing values and empower officers to engage proactively with their beats.

📊 Transparency & Accountability

Sharing data, being open about challenges, responding to community feedback, and holding ourselves accountable for both successes and failures.

Strategic Goals for 2026-2028

Goal 1: Expand Beat Integrity

Objective: Ensure officers are assigned to consistent geographic beats to build familiarity and relationships.

  • Reduce beat reassignments by 40%
  • Increase average beat tenure to 18+ months
  • Prioritize community engagement time in officer schedules
  • Measure success through community surveys and officer feedback

Goal 2: Strengthen Community Advisory Councils

Objective: Create formal structures for ongoing dialogue between police and community members.

  • Establish advisory councils in all 22 police districts
  • Hold monthly meetings open to all residents
  • Implement council recommendations into district strategies
  • Train officers in facilitation and active listening

Goal 3: Invest in Youth Engagement

Objective: Build positive relationships with young people to prevent future crime and improve perceptions of police.

  • Expand Police Athletic League (PAL) programs to all districts
  • Implement school-based mentorship initiatives
  • Create summer jobs and internship programs for teens
  • Partner with schools on violence prevention and conflict resolution

Goal 4: Problem-Oriented Policing Projects

Objective: Move beyond traditional enforcement to address underlying conditions that contribute to crime.

  • Train all officers in problem-solving methodologies
  • Dedicate time for officers to work on community-identified problems
  • Partner with city departments (streets, sanitation, housing) on quality-of-life issues
  • Document and share successful problem-solving case studies

Goal 5: Increase Community Visibility & Access

Objective: Make police more accessible and responsive to community needs.

  • Expand foot and bike patrols in high-density areas
  • Host regular "Coffee with a Cop" and community events
  • Increase social media engagement and two-way communication
  • Provide multilingual resources and translation services

Community-Led Initiatives

We recognize that the most effective crime prevention strategies come from community members themselves. The CPD supports and partners with community-led efforts including:

  • Neighborhood Watch Programs: Resident-led crime prevention and communication networks
  • Violence Interrupters: Community members trained to mediate conflicts before they escalate
  • Block Clubs: Hyperlocal organizing for safety and quality of life
  • Business Improvement Districts: Commercial corridor partnerships for safety and economic development
  • Faith-Based Outreach: Religious institutions as hubs for peace and conflict resolution

Measuring Success

Community policing success cannot be measured by arrests alone. We track progress through:

Crime & Safety Metrics

  • Reported crime trends (with focus on violent crime reduction)
  • Community perceptions of safety (quarterly surveys)
  • Problem-solving outcomes (issues resolved vs. ongoing)

Trust & Legitimacy Metrics

  • Community satisfaction with police performance
  • Trust in police fairness and respect
  • Complaint and commendation trends
  • Participation rates in community programs

Engagement Metrics

  • Number and diversity of community meetings/events
  • Officer time spent on proactive community engagement
  • Social media engagement and sentiment
  • Youth program participation

Get Involved

Community policing only works when community members actively participate. Here's how you can get involved:

  • Attend District Advisory Council Meetings: Voice your concerns and help shape police priorities
  • Join Neighborhood Watch: Connect with neighbors to keep your block safe
  • Volunteer with Youth Programs: Mentor young people and support PAL activities
  • Participate in Events: Come to Coffee with a Cop, community walks, and problem-solving sessions
  • Provide Feedback: Share your experiences and ideas through surveys and online platforms
